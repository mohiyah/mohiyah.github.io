1 Introduction

This paper presents a country study of voting behavior for the case of Serbia. Serbia makes an interesting case for several reasons: As a country with both a longstanding history (and struggle) with electoral democracy and socialist realist regime form (Guzina 2021), its democracy deals with some of the woes many young post-socialist liberal democracies struggle with: corruption, clientelism, electoral coercion, media un-freedom among others (Freedom House 2025). In light of current large student demonstrations across cities of Serbia against the long standing government of President Aleksandar Vučić’s SNS party coalition (SRF 4 News 2025), the question of the relation between dissatisfaction with the government and voting behavior gains actuality (see Baletic 2025). This short paper aims to provide insights in vote determinants of Serbia’s residents. The focus group of this paper are young people: those who currently go on the street against the government. Key questions are, whether they democratically participate only by demonstrations or whether they also vote accordingly, and further, what individual factors contribute the most to vote turnout. The study relies on the latest round of the European Social Survey (Sikt 2024).

2 Theory

Determinants of voter turnout are generally classified into socio-psychological (e.g. “motivational”), sociological, historical-institutional, rational choice and communicative factors (Lazić 2023; Hadjar and Beck 2010). Most commonly, a mix of socio-economic factors and personal value-based predictors are used to estimate voter turnout, with a recent trend to incorporate contextual factors in multilevel analyses (Bühlmann and Freitag 2006; Hadjar and Beck 2010; further Lazić 2023; Steenbergen 2010). Following individual level socio-economic predictors are commonly used. Age of respondent, as one argument is that with a higher age, people tend to have more knowledge and experience of life in general, as well as being more invested in material aspects of life (e.g. maturity), and therefore have a higher interest and capacity in voting. The other argument for age as a predictor is based on the presumption, that post-materialist generations have a different set of values and therefore see voting as less of a duty than older generations. The first argument falls under the life-cycle theory, the latter under cohort theory (Hadjar and Beck 2010).

Education is also important for two reasons: better educated people tend to have more knowledge in general, and therefore are more able to understand the complexities of a political system, and they tend to be associated with higher societal status, giving them more opportunities to engage in influential networks; both factors are expected to raise the voting propensity of an individual (Hadjar and Beck 2010).

Gender carries with it a lot of societal and socio-psychological implications, thus making an interesting potential predictor of vote outcome. Some studies suggest a lower political interest by women, potentially shrinking women’s propensity to vote (Hadjar and Beck 2010).

Income shares some characteristics of education (Hadjar and Beck 2010), potentially giving people more access to power and thus increasing the motivation to participate, however, it would be interesting to analyse the relationship between income and education regarding individual probability to vote.

Psychological, or motivational factors often stem from rational choice theories or utility theory of voting (Hadjar and Beck 2010). Political efficacy as vote turnout predictor speaks to the mechanism, that people expect a personal gain from voting when they have the belief that they have the ability to participate in and influence the political system, thus being more prone to vote (Hadjar and Beck 2010). The willingness to participate in politics both formally and informally comes with higher political interest (Hadjar and Beck 2010). Individual trust in the political system has similar mechanisms working behind it as political efficacy; people who trust the state institutions reasonably expect gains by participating in the system and are therefore expected to have a higher tendency to vote than people with low trust (Hadjar and Beck 2010). Another common predictor for vote turnout is satisfaction with political system outputs, as individuals with high satisfaction tend to participate more, viewing the political system as legitimate. On the other hand the opposite could be observed, as highly satisfied citizens wouldn’t vote because “everything works already fine” (Hadjar and Beck 2010).

Bühlmann and Freitag (2006) propose the inclusion of an additional set of predictors of vote turnout, they call it social integration factors. Individuals with higher socialization in communal networks tend to have a higher propensity to vote, as they are more socialized in communities and thus closer to political processes on the lower level. Accordingly, the longer individuals live in the same place, the more they are affected by political outcomes in this area, and thus have gain more by voting than people who often move (Bühlmann and Freitag 2006).

Regarding the long-standing findings on socio-economic factor influence on vote turnout, Lazić (2023) points to regime form as an important contextual factor. With his classification of Serbia as a “Hybrid Regime”, one in which elections are held regularly and mostly freely but significant oppression against political opponents is upheld by the ruling party, he points out that findings in vote turnout estimations might often be contradictory to findings in fully developed democracies. For example, individuals from sophisticated high education background might have a lower propensity to vote than low educated citizens (Lazić 2023).

A particularly interesting dimension in the current case of Serbia is the link between alternative forms of participating and voting. Those who participated democratically outside of voting in Serbia were more likely to vote, however, this effect was significantly weaker after the 2000 democratic elections (Pantić and Pavlović 2009). The authors(Pantić and Pavlović 2009) also mention in their study on political culture in Serbia that alternative forms of participation, such as demonstrating and signing petitions, are viewed as complementary to voting. Bernhagen and Marsh (2007) state the same for Eastern Europe as a whole with regards to protesting and voting. Non-voters are generally less active in all forms of political participation (Pantić and Pavlović 2009). Together with the previous findings, one could expect a positive relationship between protesting and voting in present day Serbia.

3 Data and Method

The data used for this analysis is collected as part of the eleventh wave of the European Social Survey (Sikt 2024). Data collection took place in late 2023 and early 2024, which is an interesting point in time in Serbia in context of this paper, due to general actuality as well as two mass shootings triggered large protests against the government and government-tied media earlier in closely before data collection took place (Fahrländer 2023). The data in the ESS data set suits the purpose of this study well for individual level factors, however, contextual factors would have to be added manually as done by Hadjar and Beck (2010). However, ESS data offers the possibility for hierarchical analysis by grouping the observations within a country with the region variable. This variable takes the values “Belgrade region”, “Region of Vojvodina”, “Region of Šumadija and Western Serbia” and “Region of South and East Serbia”. A possible problem with the ESS data arises in regards to the dependent variable of interest, namely whether an individual voted or not. ESS surveys rely on the individuals self-reporting of casting a ballot (Sikt 2024), thus being susceptible to over-reporting, as individuals might give socially desirable answers instead of the truth (Hadjar and Beck 2010). The measurement of socio-economic factors is straightforward with ESS data, as the variables of interest are measured directly, except for income, where instead of personal income household income is used as a predictor. The factor of political efficacy will be measured by combining three ESS variables which use the same Likert scale. (1) political system allows people to have a say in what government does, (2) political system allows people to have influence on politics, (3) confident in own ability to participate in politics. Predictor political interest is also straightforward, as such a question exists in the ESS questionnaire. Trust is measured for trust in legal system, police, parliament, politicians and political parties. Since the outcome variable deals with parliamentary elections, I will use the variable for trust in country’s parliament, also with the expectation that the variables somewhat correlate. In order to measure satisfaction with the political system, I’ll again combine the variables (1) how satisfied with national government and (2) how satisfied with the way democracy works in the country. The socialization factors can be measured by the ESS variable is/has been member of a trade union or similar organisation. There is no variable in the current ESS wave for duration of residency at the same place. Considering forms of alternative participation as predictors of individual voting propensity, the ESS data offers three variables: (1) Whether one wore/displayed campaign badges/stickers the last 12 months, (2) whether one signed a petition during the last 12 months and (3) whether one participated in a public demonstration in the last 12 months. I’m particularly interested in public demonstrations as stated in the introduction, thus I will only use taken part in public demonstration last 12 months.

4 Results

As we can see in Figure 1 , around 30% of values for household income are missing in the ESS sample for Serbia. These are the biggest outliers in terms of missing value proportion, followed by satisfaction with current state of democracy. Given the high missing proportion the household income variable, it makes sense to check for missing patterns with the other socio-economic predictors.

Figure 4 shows that missing values in household income are not equally distributed over respondents’ age. There is also a discrepancy between genders in reported household income. Therefore imputations, rather than listwise row deletions make sense, as they may disturb the outcome. According to Rubin’s (1976) formula \(RE = (1 + \frac{FMI}{m})^{-1}\) we can calculate the number of imputations to be 5.7 with a desired relative efficiency of 95% and fraction of missing information of 30%.

Table 1 shows the pooled coefficients from multiply imputed logistic regression models. In spite of high complexity, the full model shows the lowest AIC and BIC values compared to the restricted models. This indicates that the full model is the best fitting model. Regarding socio-economic factors, age and gender exert significant influence, with the effect of age remaining stable in effect size and significance across model variations. The result indicates a higher voting propensity with higher age, a relationship consistent with popular findings (Hadjar and Beck 2010; Bühlmann and Freitag 2006), but contrary to Lazić’s (2023). The author indicates that in Serbia younger, less educated people have a higher propensity of voting. Nonetheless, in our model, we observe a 2% increase in odds for voting for every year increase of an individuals age, holding all other variables constant, while women have 34% higher odds of participating in national elections than men. Socio-psychological factors, namely political interest and political efficacy are the only ones with stable influence on individual vote turnout; unsurprisingly, the higher an individual’s interest in politics and belief of influence in politics, the higher its propensity to vote. Satisfaction in politics, trust in parliament and one’s (former) membership in social organizations seem to have no effect on vote propensity in Serbia. Regarding the so called protest model, the results suggest a positive relationship between attending public demonstrations and propensity to vote. Holding all other factors constant, the odds of having participated in the last national elections are 4.3 times higher for someone who has recently taken part in public demonstrations compared to someone who hasn’t. This observation is in line with Pantić’s (2009) and Bernhagen and Marsh’s (2007) observations, who stated that voting and alternative forms of political participation in Serbia and Eastern Europe are predominantly viewed as complementary by individuals. Since protests were widespread in various regions of Serbia, the question remains whether the observed relationship varies in effect size across different regions.

Table 2 shows the results of the full model with standardized coefficients, taking into account the respondents’ origins from different regions in Serbia. The results don’t change in terms of significance of particular coefficients. Crucially, a very low ICC indicates heterogeneity of the collected data across all regions, indicating that the observed relationships don’t vary significantly depending on a respondents origin within Serbia, thus making a multilevel model not necessary.

Main findings from Table 1 are visually presented in Figure 5. Two things stand out: The significant influence of participation in public demonstrations on individual voter turnout and the overall high level of voting probability. The latter observation points to a contradiction to Lazić’s (2023) findings, who describes low voter turnout in the 21st century as symptomatic for Serbia and other post-communist democracies.

5 Discussion

Taking into account both socio-economic and socio-psychological (e.g. “motivational”) factors, a strong relationship between alternative forms of political participation, namely taking part in public demonstrations, and individual vote propensity can be observed. The latest protests in Serbia do not present a substituting form of pol. participation, but rather a complementary one, across all age groups, genders, and political interest levels. This finding supports previous ones by Bernhagen and Marsh (2007) and Pantić and Pavlović (2009). The effects do not vary significantly across different regions of Serbia. Important caveats need to be mentioned; as the outcome variable relied on self reporting, there might be a bias towards reporting to having voted by people who already have a high interest in politics, a problem addressed by Hadjar and Beck (2010). One could consequently imagine over-reporting by protest participants, potentially posing a similar bias.

tidy_mi <- function(model, model_name) {
  model %>%
    mutate(
      term = rownames(model),
      model = model_name,
      stars = case_when(
        `P-value` < 0.001 ~ "***",
        `P-value` < 0.01  ~ "**",
        `P-value` < 0.05  ~ "*",
        `P-value` < 0.1  ~ "+",
        TRUE ~ ""
      ),
      coef_fmt = paste0(round(coef, 2), stars, "\n(", round(SE, 2), ")")
    ) %>%
    select(term, model, coef_fmt)
}

results <- bind_rows(

  aics <- sapply(fit$analyses, AIC)
  bics <- sapply(fit$analyses, BIC)

@tbl-models1 shows the pooled coefficients from multiply imputed logistic regression models. In spite of high complexity, the full model shows the lowest AIC and BIC values compared to the restricted models. This indicates that the full model is the best fitting model. Regarding socio-economic factors, age and gender exert significant influence, with the effect of age remaining stable in effect size and significance across model variations. The result indicates a higher voting propensity with higher age, a relationship consistent with popular findings [@hadjar2010; @buhlmannIndividualContextualDeterminants2006], but contrary to Lazić's [-@lazic2023]. The author indicates that in Serbia younger, less educated people have a higher propensity of voting. Nonetheless, in our model, we observe a 2% increase in odds for voting for every year increase of an individuals age, holding all other variables constant, while women have 34% higher odds of participating in national elections than men. Socio-psychological factors, namely political interest and political efficacy are the only ones with stable influence on individual vote turnout; unsurprisingly, the higher an individual's interest in politics and belief of influence in politics, the higher its propensity to vote. Satisfaction in politics, trust in parliament and one's (former) membership in social organizations seem to have no effect on vote propensity in Serbia. Regarding the so called protest model, the results suggest a positive relationship between attending public demonstrations and propensity to vote. Holding all other factors constant, the **odds of having participated in the last national elections are 4.3 times higher** for someone who has recently taken part in public demonstrations compared to someone who hasn't. This observation is in line with Pantić's [-@pantic2009] and Bernhagen and Marsh's [-@bernhagenVotingProtestingExplaining2007] observations, who stated that voting and alternative forms of political participation in Serbia and Eastern Europe are predominantly viewed as complementary by individuals. \newline Since protests were widespread in various regions of Serbia, the question remains whether the observed relationship varies in effect size across different regions.

get_icc <- function(fit) {
  analyses_list <- mice::getfit(fit)  # Proper way to extract analyses
  var_region <- sapply(analyses_list, function(x) {
    as.data.frame(lme4::VarCorr(x))$vcov[1]
  })
  icc <- var_region / (var_region + (pi^2 / 3))
  return(icc)
}

icc_vals <- get_icc(mlm_fit)
icc_mean <- round(mean(icc_vals), 3)

tidy_mi_mlm <- function(model, model_name, var_labels = NULL) {

  pooled_model <- pool(model)
  
  out <- broom.mixed::tidy(pooled_model, effects = "fixed") %>%
    mutate(
      term = ifelse(!is.null(var_labels) & term %in% names(var_labels),
                    var_labels[term], term),
      stars = case_when(
        p.value < 0.001 ~ "***",
        p.value < 0.01  ~ "**",
        p.value < 0.05  ~ "*",
        p.value < 0.1   ~ "+",
        TRUE ~ ""
      ),
      coef_fmt = paste0(round(estimate, 2), stars, "\n(", round(std.error, 2), ")"),
      model = model_name
    ) %>%
    select(term, model, coef_fmt)

  return(out)
}

## Discussion

Taking into account both socio-economic and socio-psychological (e.g. "motivational") factors, a strong relationship between alternative forms of political participation, namely taking part in public demonstrations, and individual vote propensity can be observed. The latest protests in Serbia do not present a substituting form of pol. participation, but rather a complementary one, across all age groups, genders, and political interest levels. This finding supports previous ones by Bernhagen and Marsh [-@bernhagenVotingProtestingExplaining2007] and Pantić and Pavlović [-@pantic2009]. The effects do not vary significantly across different regions of Serbia. Important caveats need to be mentioned; as the outcome variable relied on self reporting, there might be a bias towards reporting to having voted by people who already have a high interest in politics, a problem addressed by Hadjar and Beck [-@hadjar2010]. One could consequently imagine over-reporting by protest participants, potentially posing a similar bias.
